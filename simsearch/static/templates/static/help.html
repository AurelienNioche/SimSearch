Using similarity search

Similarity search is designed to help you find a kanji you don't know, by using a similar one as a query. For example, suppose you ran into the kanji 動, which you hadn't seen before. It looks a lot like one you do know, 働 from 働く hataraku "to work". So, you enter 働 as query, and can quickly find the unknown kanji.

If you enter a query, and can't immediately see the kanji you're looking for, you should click on the next closest match. By doing this a few times, you should hopefully find the kanji you're looking for.

Whilst this form of search is designed for single-kanji queries, you can also use it for words by querying any of the kanji in the word. When you find your target, its translation page provides single-click queries to word-level dictionaries.

Other good dictionaries

Whilst searching this way can be fun, it's not appropriate all the time. You might not know any similar kanji to the one you're trying to find, or you might just have a difficult one. The best strategy in general is to have a number of dictionaries you can use, and to pick the best one for the job. We recommend:

  • Forgiving Online Kanji Search
    A word-level dictionary interface for search-by-pronunciation which can correct for common misreadings.
  • WWWJDIC
    The reference-level free word-level dictionary for Japanese, which accepts roomaji input, and occasionally has examples of use.
  • Kansuke
    A kanji-level dictionary based on a simplified method of counting strokes.
  • Handwritten kanji lookup
    Ben Bullock's handwritten kanji interface, which lets you find a kanji by drawing it.
